CRISPR Cas9

CRISPR Cas9 is a tool for gene editing, which was discovered through microbiological research. It changed our understanding of the genes that control the rapid growth and survival of cancer cells. In the future, it may help with cancer diagnosis, treatment, or prevention.

Histones

Histones are proteins that structurally support chromosomes and control gene activity. Research into these proteins and the impact of their gene modifications on the expression of genes has revealed its role on pediatric cancer. Specifically, in most cases of the pediatric brain tumor diffuse intrinsic pontine gliomas (DIPG) , mutations in the genes for a specific histone protein prevented a modification, which makes it a driver mutation for the tumor.

Immunotherapies

Immunotherapy is a treatment that uses immune-system boosting drugs to fight cancer. Immunotherapy research has led to the development of new treatment methods in the past few years. One new treatment is chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) T-cell therapy, which has helped pediatric leukemia and lymphoma patients in various clinical trials.

Challenges

  • Rarity of Childhood Cancer
  • Unkown Causes
  • Differences between Adult and Childhood Cancers
  • Considerations with Childhood Treatment

Rarity of Childhood Cancer

Cancer is rare in children, who account for less than 1% of all new US cancer diagnoses each year. As a result, pediatric cancer patients are spread across many institutions, restricting the number of available patients at clinical trials. To overcome this, cancer research institutions and children’s hospitals need to collaborate in multi-institutional studies.

Unknown Causes

The causes of childhood cancers are unknown. While a small percentage is genetic and inherited from parents, most cancers are caused by random events in the cells. Also, unlike with adult cancers, environmental exposures are difficult to identify because it is difficult to pinpoint what the children were exposed to at that stage in their lives. So, research into preventative measures is difficult for childhood cancers.

Differences between Adult and Childhood Cancers

Children and adults are generally affected by different types of cancers, and the biologies of the cancers are different. These differences prevent the use of treatments of adult cancers for children. For example, small genetic differences between the cancer cells make genetically-targeted treatments designed for adult cancers ineffective. Also, drugs that target specific pathways in adults may disrupt signaling pathways needed for development, making them difficult to use. So, childhood cancers need their own research, even independent of similar adult cancers.

Considerations with Childhood Treatment

Because of children’s development, there are important considerations when creating treatments. One large issue is developing less toxic treatments. Because of intense cancer treatments, children who have or recovered from cancer have adverse side effects, which can last their entire lives. Future treatments need to consider this and attempt to decrease the incidence of acute and late side effects.
