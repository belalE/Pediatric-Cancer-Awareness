Pediatric Cancer

Pediatric cancer is a large issue, and it requires its own research and effort, separate from adult cancers. In children ages 1 to 14, cancer is the 2nd leading cause of death after accidents. In 2021, an estimated 1,190 children under 15 years old are expected to die from cancer, and an additional 10,500 will be diagnosed with it. While modern technology and medical innovations have improved the survival of pediatric cancer patients (which dropped 65% from 1970 to 2016), further research is still necessary, especially research directed towards childhood cancers.

What makes it different from adult cancer?

Cancers that affect children are very different from those that affect adults. They are different types of cancers because of the different causes; many adult cancers are caused by lifestyle and environment. Consequently, their biologies are very different, making some treatments ineffective against childhood cancers. Also, children typically respond better to treatment because of the difference in the types of cancer, the higher intensity of the treatments, and the fact that children often don't have other health issues. Because of these differences, treatments for childhood cancers require their own research. With these developing treatments, side effects are a greater concern because children’s bodies are developing, and most current treatments cause long-term side effects.

Causes

The causes of pediatric cancer are mostly unknown. Only about 5% of childhood cancers are genetic, or caused by inherited mutations. However, most cancers are not caused by inherited mutations, but by gene mutations early in the child’s life. It is difficult to identify the causes of these mutations for children, unlike for adults. Lifestyle causes, which apply for adults, don’t apply for children because they take many years to take effect. Also, with environmental causes, it is difficult to determine what carcinogens they were exposed to at such a young age. Some studies have reported various causes, although they are not strong enough to draw firm conclusions. Some examples of those causes are from parental exposure, like parent’s tobacco smoking, exposure at their workplace, or pesticides used around the home. Still, even with these findings, many cancers are caused by random events in the cell without any outside influence.

Treatments

  • Surgery - During surgery, surgeons either remove or debulk a tumor. Debulking a tumor is when surgeons remove some of a tumor but not all of it in order to prevent damage to an organ or the body. Debulking a tumor improves the success of other treatments. Surgery can not be used to treat leukemia, which occurs in the blood, or metastasized cancer (cancer that spreads to other areas of the body).
  • Chemotherapy is a treatment that uses drugs to stop or slow cancer growth by killing rapidly growing cells. It is often used with other treatments, such as to shrink a tumor before surgery.
  • Radiation Therapy uses high dose radiation to kill and shrink tumors. It does this by damaging the cells’ DNA, which causes them to stop dividing or die.
  • Immunotherapy is a treatment that uses immune-system boosting drugs to fight cancer. The treatment can involve creating T-cells or antibodies designed to target specific cancer cells, administering vaccines, or inhibiting immune system checkpoints.
  • Stem cell transplants are not treatments themselves, rather it is a way to recover from other treatments. This involves transplanting stem cells into bone marrow, taken from either the patient, a relative, or another person. It is often used with leukemia and lymphoma patients.
